Research

My research investigates the neural mechanisms underlying decision-making vulnerabilities in aging, with the ultimate goal of developing targeted interventions to protect older adults from exploitation and misinformation. Through my Ph.D. training in Social Psychology at Temple University, I have developed expertise at the intersection of neuroscience, psychology, and computational modeling to understand how age-related cognitive changes create susceptibility to harmful outcomes like financial exploitation and confirmation bias.

Research Background

My research journey began with investigating moral decision-making during my master's degree with Dr. Andrew Monroe at Appalachian State University. This work, which earned first place in the university's thesis competition, demonstrated how individual differences in moral values predict real-world behaviors and judgments. A transformative summer research experience in Dr. Catherine Hartley's developmental neuroscience lab at NYU introduced me to neuroimaging methods and sparked my interest in understanding the brain mechanisms underlying judgment and decision-making across the lifespan.

Current Research Program

As a doctoral student in Dr. David Smith's Neuroeconomics Laboratory at Temple University, I have become expert in functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), computational modeling, and advanced statistical analysis techniques. My research program has systematically characterized how individual differences in reward sensitivity, cognitive biases, and age-related neural changes contribute to decision-making vulnerabilities. This work has resulted in multiple first-author publications, competitive grant funding including an NIH F31 fellowship ($106,980) and SRNDNA pilot grant ($24,000), and presentations at major international conferences.

Brain Stimulation Research

Recognizing the limitations of purely correlational neuroimaging approaches, my current research incorporates transcranial electrical stimulation (tACS and tDCS) to establish causal links between prefrontal cortex function and decision-making processes. My dissertation project represents a novel integration of brain stimulation, computational modeling, and aging research to test whether targeted neural interventions can improve reward-based learning in older adults. This work combines Win-Stay-Lose-Shift and Rescorla-Wagner computational models with individual differences in brain structure to identify optimal candidates for cognitive enhancement interventions.

Technical Skills & Methodologies

Through my training, I have developed a unique skillset combining experimental psychology, advanced neuroimaging methods, brain stimulation techniques, computational modeling (Python, R, MATLAB), and translational research approaches. My work bridges basic neuroscience with urgent societal problems, positioning me to contribute to both theoretical understanding of cognitive aging and practical interventions for vulnerable populations.

Research Vision

My long-term research goal is to develop personalized, neuroscience-based interventions that preserve cognitive independence and protect decision-making capacity throughout the lifespan. I aim to create practical solutions that can be implemented in healthcare settings to protect vulnerable older adults from financial exploitation and other forms of manipulation.
